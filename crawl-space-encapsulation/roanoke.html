Roanoke Conditions

Why Roanoke Crawl Spaces Stay Wet

The City of Roanoke sits on the floor of a mountain valley where humid air settles and lingers. With an average annual humidity near 68% and summer afternoons climbing past 80%, a vented crawl space draws that moisture in through its foundation vents day and night. The region's dense red clay soil drains slowly and holds water against foundation walls long after Roanoke's 41 inches of yearly rain.

Geography compounds it. Homes near the Roanoke River and its tributaries, and in lower-lying parts of Southeast Roanoke and along Peters Creek, contend with a high water table and occasional flooding that pushes water directly into the crawl space. Meanwhile, a large share of Roanoke's homes, especially in Raleigh Court, Wasena, Grandin Village, South Roanoke, and Old Southwest, were built before 1980 with open dirt-floor crawl spaces and thin or missing vapor barriers that have long since failed.

The pattern we find under Roanoke homes is consistent: mold on the floor joists, a musty smell that rises into the living space, sagging or bouncy floors over moisture-rotted subfloor, and higher energy bills from an HVAC system fighting damp air. Encapsulation fixes the root cause by sealing moisture out for good.
